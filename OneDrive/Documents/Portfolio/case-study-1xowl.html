Case Study · Gaming Platform · 2024

Designing for high-stakes fun: earning trust before the first ₹50 deposit.

1X OWL is a real-money Lottery + Ludo platform targeting casual gamers in India. This is the story of how I designed an experience that converts skepticism into confidence.

01 — Problem

Real-money gaming fails when users don't trust it.

The platform had two games — Lottery and Ludo — and a product that was technically functional. The problem wasn't the game mechanics. The problem was that no one would deposit money into an app they just discovered.

Unlike Dream11 or MPL, 1X OWL had zero brand recognition, no reviews, and no social proof. The existing UI looked like every other generic gaming app — loud colors, aggressive CTAs, and no signal that real people actually got paid.

"The design challenge wasn't 'make it look good.' It was: how do we make a zero-trust product feel trustworthy enough to get a user's first ₹50?"

02 — Research

I started with trust, not tasks.

Rather than mapping the existing flow, I started by understanding the emotional state of a first-time user of a real-money app. I ran 6 user interviews (ages 20–30, Tier-2 cities), a 34-response survey, and audited 5 competitor apps.

Finding Design Implication
68% check withdrawal reviews before installing Trust signals must appear before gameplay — on the home screen
KYC feels like a trap ("they'll block my money") Reframe KYC as "Unlock your winnings" — benefit-first language
"Is my Ludo opponent a real person or a bot?" Show opponent name, avatar, and last-active status in lobby
Lottery players want to feel "smart" not "lucky" Show odds and draw history — transparency over hype

03 — Pain Points

Five problems, one root cause: no trust.

  • 01 Distrust on first open. No proof of payouts, no community signals, no brand credibility on the home screen.
  • 02 Navigation confusion. Users didn't know where to start — Lottery or Ludo felt like two separate apps crammed together.
  • 03 Transaction anxiety. Deposit and withdrawal screens felt like fintech forms — cold, unclear, with no feedback loop.
  • 04 Gameplay opacity. No live feedback during Ludo games — is it lagging, or did I lose? No in-game status communication.
  • 05 Dashboard overload. Too many numbers competing for attention. Users couldn't find their balance or next action quickly.
04 — Design Decisions

Every decision had a reason. Here are five that mattered.

1
Trust-first home screen

Replaced the "WIN BIG 🎉" hero banner with a live stats strip: X active users · ₹Y total payouts · Z avg. withdrawal time. Real numbers surface immediately.

Why: Research showed users check payout proof before playing. If we don't surface it on the first screen, they leave to find it on Google — and don't come back.
2
KYC reframed as "Secure your winnings"

Redesigned KYC as a 3-step card flow with a progress bar. Each step shows the user benefit, not just the compliance requirement. Step 1: "Verify email → Start playing". Step 3: "Add UPI → Withdraw instantly."

Why: KYC drop-off is a known death zone in fintech apps. Users feel trapped, not helped. Reframing it as unlocking a benefit reduced cognitive resistance in testing.
3
Persistent mode toggle (Lottery ⇄ Ludo)

Replaced a 5-tab bottom nav with a persistent pill toggle at the top. One tap switches context. Bottom nav stays minimal: Home, Wallet, Profile.

Why: Early wireframes showed a multi-tab nav caused users to feel lost between games. A single toggle anchors their mental model — "I am in Lottery mode right now."
4
Live presence indicators in Ludo lobby

Opponent card shows: name, avatar, city, last active "2 min ago." A subtle green dot on the profile confirms live status.

Why: "Is this a bot?" was the #1 concern in Ludo. Presence indicators answer the question without the user needing to ask it — invisible reassurance.
5
Withdrawal as a celebration moment

Post-withdrawal screen: confetti animation, "₹500 sent to your UPI in 4 min" with exact timestamp and UPI ID confirmation. A "Share your win" CTA to WhatsApp.

Why: Every successful withdrawal is a free marketing event. A user who shares their win on WhatsApp is your most credible ad. The screen was designed with that downstream behavior in mind.
05 — Process

From FigJam boxes to production-ready screens.

The design went through three distinct phases. Each phase had a clear goal — this wasn't iterating for the sake of it.

Phase 1
Low-fi Flows
FigJam sketches, user journeys
Phase 2
Mid-fi Wireframes
B&W Figma, spacing, IA decisions
Phase 3
High-fi UI
Color, type, components, prototype

Key pivot: V1 had a 5-tab bottom nav. After watching 3 users get confused between "Lottery" and "Games" tabs, I collapsed everything into a 3-tab nav + mode switcher. This was the single biggest usability improvement of the project.

06 — Design System & Visuals

Designed for Trust. Built for Speed.

To convince a user to deposit real money securely, we avoided the traditional "flashy red casino" aesthetics entirely. Instead, the design system borrows heavily from modern banking apps, anchoring on elegant purples and structured, legible typography.

Frontend Structure

Rather than forcing users to download a massive app from an App Store, we engineered 1X OWL as a highly optimized web-app (PWA). This means players just click a link and it instantly loads like a native app on their phone. We also integrated a powerful push-notification system so users instantly receive an alert on their phone the second they win a lottery draw.

08 — Reflection

What I'd do differently.

If I were to restart this project, I'd run a proper card-sorting session before finalizing the IA — I made some navigation decisions based on competitive analysis that could have been validated faster with users.

I'd also build a more formal design system from week 1. Components evolved organically, which created technical debt in the Figma file by week 7. Clean systems make for faster iterations — I learned that the hard way.
